Turnover Analysis

Employee turnover is higher among newer employees. Employees with a tenure of 25 and under totaled 571, compared to 558 employees with a tenure of 26 and above. Newer employees experienced a turnover rate of 53%, while long-tenured employees had a lower turnover rate of 48%, representing a 5% difference.
Female employees show a slightly higher turnover rate at 51%, compared to 49% for male employees. It is important to note that females represent the majority of the workforce, accounting for 76% (853 employees) of the total employee population, while males account for 24% (276 employees).
The recruitment channel associated with the highest turnover is employee referrals. Referral hires experienced a turnover rate of 66%, with 63 out of 95 employees leaving the organization.
The age group with the highest turnover rate is 18–28, with a turnover rate of 55%. A total of 252 employees left out of 459 employees within this age range.
The Building industry has the highest turnover rate at 76%, with 31 out of 41 employees leaving roles within this industry.

Key Findings & Recommendations

Tenure & Turnover: Newer employees (≤25 tenure) experience higher turnover than long-tenured employees. The data suggests that employees are most likely to leave after onboarding but before establishing long-term commitment.
Gender & Turnover: Female employees have a slightly higher turnover rate (51%) compared to male employees (49%). However, females represent the majority of the workforce, accounting for 76% (853 employees), while males represent 24% (276 employees).
Recruitment Channel: Referral hires show the highest turnover rate at 66%. This suggests potential mismatches in role expectations or lower long-term commitment among referral-based hires, contrary to typical hiring assumptions.
Age Range: Employees aged 18–28 have the highest turnover rate. Early-career employees are more likely to job-hop, explore alternative opportunities, or leave when career growth and development paths are unclear.
